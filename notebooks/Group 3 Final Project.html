SEC Firms and their Relationship with High Net Worth Clients¶

Group 3(Money Magnets): Dhanya Menon, Tim Jacobs, Jeff Leonard, Chloe McCabe, Abhineu Pradhan¶

Investment firms play an integral role in building their clients' wealth, guiding them in achieving long-term financial goals and navigating complex financial situations. The SEC identifies a high-net-worth individual as an “Accredited Investor”. Someone who has a net worth of at least 1 million or an income of at least 200,000 per year. They could also be someone who holds a financial professional license. For this project, we put ourselves in the shoes of an Investment Advisor planning to start a new business focused on High Net Worth Clients (HNWIs). We asked ourselves some key questions to guide our approach: Where should we set up our office? Where are high net worth clients geographically located? How should we structure our fees? And what level of assets under management should we aim for?

Our analysis looks at data available from US. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) to analyze how the distribution of high-net-worth individuals (HNWI) as well as the distribution of strategies used by investment advising firms vary widely based on firm size, location, and compensation structure.

Link: https://www.sec.gov/data-research/sec-markets-data/information-about-registered-investment-advisers-exempt-reporting-advisers

Conclusion¶

Main Question: What defines the successful investment advisor for High Net Worth clients?¶

Sub-questions:¶

1. Where should they be located?¶
2. What level of AUM should they target?¶
3. How should they structure their fees?¶

The main question we were trying to answer is listed above followed by the three smaller questions. This analysis provided a data-driven perspective of what factors define a sucessful investment firm for High Net Worth Clients. Based on our findings, if you want to serve a high number of HNW clients, states like Missouri and Massachusetts seemed like ideal locations. However, New York and California, had high concentrations of HNW investor wealth. So if firms were targeting a low number of clients but a high dollar value, they should aim to set up offices in New York or California. Firms should aim to maintain at least $8 billion in assets to remain competitive. In terms of fee structure, firms should adopt an AUM-based compensation model as this aligns with client expectations and industry standards. Focusing on this fee strategy, size and strategic location, startup advisory firms can build a strong portfolio of wealthy clients and thrive in the competitive financial advisory landscape.

Interactive tools in the analysis allowed us to explore how firm attributes such as RAUM, employee size and compensation model influence the best location to have an office. To summarize, the data findings indicate that a successful investment advisor for HNW client is shaped by a balanced mix of scale, specialization and compensation structure and not just location alone.

  • Handling large data file in Jupyter Notebook: one set of data was in Excel format and the other was in pdf. So we spent a lot of time collaborating to find the right columns for our analysis. But we successfully accomplished this.
  • Converting large numbers from scientific notation to integer format.
  • Resolving Number formatting inconsistencies between CSV file and Jupyter Notebook.
  • Transforming "Y"/"N" variables for data aggregation which then could be combined.
  • Categorizing each model individually to allow analysis of each category.
  • Combining two charts into histogram format with different scales proved challenging so we eventually proceeded histograms in seaborn to avoid the problem yet achieve the desired purpose.
